Interactive E-Learning Module | Articulate Rise 360

Context

This self-paced e-learning course was designed to onboard educators transitioning into adult high school equivalency instruction. The primary challenge was helping licensed K-12 teachers adapt their pedagogical approaches to serve adult learners in correctional and community education settings, while building confidence in teaching unfamiliar content areas like social studies and science at the high school equivalency level.

Target Audience: Adult education instructors, former K-12 educators, GED/HiSET coordinators, adult literacy volunteers

Duration: 6 lessons, 2-3 hours total

Tool: Articulate Rise 360

Learning Outcomes

Upon completion, educators will be able to:

  1. Differentiate between HiSET and GED exam structures, formats, and requirements
  2. Apply appropriate instructional strategies for each HiSET content area
  3. Address unique challenges faced by adult learners in HiSET preparation
  4. Implement effective assessment and practice test strategies
  5. Locate and customize HiSET-specific teaching materials
  6. Support students in post-HiSET educational and career planning

Instructional Design Decisions

Adult Learning Principles Applied:

  • Self-Paced Flexibility: Designed for busy professionals who need to fit training around existing work schedules
  • Immediate Relevance: Every lesson connects directly to real teaching scenarios educators will face
  • Confidence Building: Explicitly addresses teacher anxiety about teaching unfamiliar content

Interactive Elements:

  • Knowledge checks for immediate feedback
  • Scenario-based learning with authentic teaching situations
  • Drag-and-drop activities for categorizing exam formats
  • Multimedia integration (video, infographics, audio)
  • Downloadable resource library with templates and checklists

Outcomes & Impact

Program Application: Currently in use for onboarding new instructors at Richmond Correctional Institution's HiSET program, which achieved:

Learning Module Design

The Great Jewish-Roman War: Historical Analysis & Critical Thinking

Learning Module for Adult High School Equivalency Preparation

Context

Adult learners in correctional education settings often struggle with social studies content that feels disconnected from their lives. This module teaches historical analysis skills, cause-and-effect reasoning, and primary source evaluation through the compelling narrative of the Jewish-Roman War (66-73 CE)â€”a conflict offering clear parallels to modern geopolitical tensions.

Target Audience: Adult learners (ages 22-55) preparing for HiSET social studies exam

Duration: 3-4 class sessions (90-120 minutes total)

Learning Objectives

  1. Analyze cause-and-effect relationships in historical events
  2. Evaluate primary sources and identify bias in historical narratives
  3. Compare historical and contemporary conflicts
  4. Construct historical arguments using evidence from multiple sources

Why This Topic?

The Jewish-Roman War offers multiple entry points for engagement:

  • Power dynamics: Occupation, resistance, and rebellion resonate with students who have experienced institutional authority
  • Moral complexity: No clear "good guys" or "bad guys"
  • Modern parallels: Connections to contemporary conflicts without overtly political framing
  • Dramatic narrative: Inherently compelling story that maintains student interest

Scaffolding Strategy

The module uses a carefully sequenced progression from concrete to abstract:

  • Session 1: Historical context & narrative (building foundational knowledge)
  • Session 2: Primary source analysis (evaluating evidence and recognizing bias)
  • Session 3: Cause & effect analysis (understanding complex historical causation)
  • Session 4: Contemporary connections & assessment (transfer and application)

Outcomes & Impact

  • 95% of students who completed the module successfully passed historical analysis questions on HiSET practice tests
  • Students reported higher confidence with primary source analysis
  • Module design adapted for other historical topics (American Revolution, Civil Rights Movement)

Information Architecture

Creative Writing Opportunity Database

Information Architecture & User Experience Design

ðŸ“Š View Database

Context

Graduate creative writing students face a persistent problem: finding relevant submission opportunities scattered across hundreds of literary magazines, contests, residencies, and fellowships is time-consuming and inefficient. This project created a centralized, filterable database of 975+ creative writing opportunities to streamline the discovery process.

Target Users: Creative writing students (undergraduate through PhD), faculty advisors, independent writers

Team: Collaborative project with graduate writing program colleagues

Tool: Google Sheets with advanced filtering

Information Architecture

Core Fields:

  • Organization Name: Hyperlinked to official website
  • Deadline/Dates: Rolling deadlines, specific dates, seasonal windows
  • Opportunity Type: Residency, Publication, Contest, Fellowship, Workshop, Grant
  • Audience: Undergraduate, Graduate, MFA, PhD, All Levels
  • Genre: Poetry, Fiction, Nonfiction, All Genres, Playwriting, Screenwriting
  • Additional Info: Location, prize amounts, fees, special requirements

User Experience Design

Filter System:

  • Single-select filters for Audience and Opportunity Type
  • Multi-select filter for Genre (writers work across forms)
  • Chronological sorting for deadline prioritization
  • Built-in instructions embedded in spreadsheet

Problem-Solution Mapping

Problem: "There are too many literary magazinesâ€”I don't know where to start."
Solution: Genre and Audience filters narrow 975 opportunities to manageable subsets

Problem: "I found the perfect contest but the deadline passed last week."
Solution: Sortable deadline column allows filtering by date range

Problem: "I spent an hour researching a residency that's for visual artists only."
Solution: Clear categorization provides quick screening before detailed research

Impact

  • Database Scale: 975+ opportunities cataloged
  • Search Efficiency: Average discovery time reduced from 2-3 hours to 15-20 minutes
